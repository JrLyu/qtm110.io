Bayesian Inference and Base Rates

Probability Overview

Probability: the probability of an event AAA is the chance that AAA occurs. We denote it as P(A)P(A)P(A).

  • P(A)∈[0,1]=∈[0%,100%]P(A)\in[0,1]=\in[0\%,100\%]P(A)∈[0,1]=∈[0%,100%]

Probability Space: a probability space is a set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. We denote it as SSS.

  • Sample space SSS = total area. So, P(S)=1=100%P(S)=1=100\%P(S)=1=100%
  • We use Venn Diagram to depict probabilities graphically.

Independent Events: two events AAA and BBB are independent if they are unrelated. Independent events satisfy the following formula for their joint probability: P(A∩B)=P(A)⋅P(B). P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B). P(A∩B)=P(A)⋅P(B).

Conditional Probability: the probability of an event AAA given that another event BBB has occurred. We denote it as P(A∣B)P(A\mid B)P(A∣B).

  • To calculate conditional probability, we have the following formula: P(A∣B)=P(A∩B)P(B). P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}. P(A∣B)=​P(B)​​P(A∩B)​​.
  • The notation $\mid$ is called a pipe. It is read as "given that" or "conditional on".

Joint Probability: the probability of two events AAA and BBB occurring together. We denote it as P(A∩B)P(A\cap B)P(A∩B).

  • To calculate joint probability, we have the following formula: Joint Probability=P(A∩B)=P(A∣B)⋅P(B). \text{Joint Probability}=P(A\cap B)=P(A\mid B)\cdot P(B). Joint Probability=P(A∩B)=P(A∣B)⋅P(B).
  • Law of Total Probability: If we have an event BBB that takes on nnn values and an event AAA conditional on it, then P(a)=∑nP(A∣Bn)⋅P(Bn). P(a)=\sum_{n}P(A\mid B_n)\cdot P(B_n). P(a)=​n​∑​​P(A∣B​n​​)⋅P(B​n​​).
    • This is a simple-weighted average.

Flipping Conditional Probability

  • Bayes' Rule: a formula that allows us to flip conditional probabilities: P(B∣A)=P(A∣B)⋅P(B)P(A) P(B\mid A)=\dfrac{P(A\mid B)\cdot P(B)}{P(A)} P(B∣A)=​P(A)​​P(A∣B)⋅P(B)​​

Bayes' Rule

  • Bayes' Rule and Binary Events.
    • Binary events: only two potential outcomes
    • When we have binary events, it is useful to re-write P(B)P(B)P(B) as P(B)=P(B∣A)P(A)+P(B∣¬A)P(¬A), P(B)=P(B\mid A)P(A)+P(B\mid\neg A)P(\neg A), P(B)=P(B∣A)P(A)+P(B∣¬A)P(¬A), where ¬A\neg A¬A means "not AAA".
    • Then, Bayes' Rule becomes P(A∣B)=P(B∣A)P(A)P(B∣A)P(A)+P(B∣¬A)P(¬A) P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(B\mid A)P(A)}{P(B\mid A)P(A)+P(B\mid\neg A)P(\neg A)} P(A∣B)=​P(B∣A)P(A)+P(B∣¬A)P(¬A)​​P(B∣A)P(A)​​

Medical Testing

  • Positive Test Results
    • False Positive Rate of 5%: If you don't have the disease, the test correctly return a negative result 95% of the time.
      • P(−∣don’t have disease)=95%P(-\mid\text{don't have disease})=95\%P(−∣don’t have disease)=95%
      • P(+∣don’t have disease)=5%false positiveP(+\mid\text{don't have disease})=5\%\quad\text{false positive}P(+∣don’t have disease)=5%false positive
      • In statistics: Type I error
    • False Negative Rate of 5%: If you do not have the disease, the test correctly returns a positive result 5% of the time.
      • P(+∣have disease)=95%P(+\mid\text{have disease})=95\%P(+∣have disease)=95%
      • P(−∣have disease)=5%false negativeP(-\mid\text{have disease})=5\%\quad\text{false negative}P(−∣have disease)=5%false negative
      • In statistics: Type II error
  • Application:
    • Given P(+∣have)P(+\mid\text{have})P(+∣have) and P(+∣don’t)P(+\mid\text{don't})P(+∣don’t). Find P(have disease∣+)P(\text{have disease}\mid+)P(have disease∣+)
    • By Bayes' Rule, we have P(have∣+)=P(+∣have)×P(have)P(+∣have)×P(have)+P(+∣don’t)×P(don’t) P(\text{have}\mid+)=\dfrac{P(+\mid\text{have})\times P(\text{have})}{P(+\mid\text{have})\times P(\text{have})+P(+\mid\text{don't})\times P(\text{don't})} P(have∣+)=​P(+∣have)×P(have)+P(+∣don’t)×P(don’t)​​P(+∣have)×P(have)​​
    • So, we will need to know P(have)P(\text{have})P(have) and P(don’t)P(\text{don't})P(don’t).

Information, Beliefs, Priors, and Posteriors

  • Bayes' Theorem in Use:
    • Prior: our beliefs, such as P(have)P(\text{have})P(have) for having the disease
    • Evidence: the new information we have, such as P(+∣have)P(+\mid\text{have})P(+∣have), positive test result
    • Posterio: update our beliefs based on the evidence, such as P(have∣+)P(\text{have}\mid+)P(have∣+)
  • Balancing prior and evidence
    • The new information seems damming, but the prior pushes in the other direction.
    • But now that we know that we are in one of these extremely unlikely scenarios.
    • We have to ask about the relative likelihood of each one.
  • Bayes' Rates:
    • Sometimes, we will use the term bayes' rates to refer to the priors.
    • In order to compute the quantity of interest, P(∣A)P(\mid A)P(∣A), we need to know the base rate of AAA, P(A)P(A)P(A).
    • Lots of analysts draw incorrect inferences because they fail to consider the base rate.

Bayes' Theorem and Quantitative Analysis

  • Bayes' Theorem and Quantitative Analysis
    • Most quantitative analysts conduct hypothesis test and compute p-values.
      • We've already discussed some reasons why the p-value might lead us astray.
      • But any given study is not all that we know about something.
    • When an analyst finds a low p-value and concludes that their finding must be true, they've made the same mistake as the mathematician and the prosecutor in People v. Collins.
      • They calculated P(estimate∣no effect)P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{no effect})P(estimate∣no effect)
        • What's the chance that I have an estimate of this size given there was no effect
        • The probability of bad luck driving my result
      • However, we really want to compute P(no effect∣estimate)P(\text{no effect}\mid\text{estimate})P(no effect∣estimate)
        • What's the probability that there is no effect given my estimate.
  • Bayes' and Estimated Effect
    • Suppose we've analyzed some data and obtained a statistically significant effect at the .05 level. (i.e., p<.05)
    • What's the probability that the estimated effect is genuine? - Bayes' Theorem! P(effect∣estimate)=P(estimate∣effect)×P(effect)P(estimate∣effect)×P(effect)+P(estimate∣no effect)×[1−P(effect)] P(\text{effect}\mid\text{estimate})=\frac{P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{effect})\times {P(\text{effect})}}{P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{effect})\times P(\text{effect})+P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{no effect})\times [1-P(\text{effect})]} P(effect∣estimate)=​P(estimate∣effect)×P(effect)+P(estimate∣no effect)×[1−P(effect)]​​P(estimate∣effect)×P(effect)​​
      • We know P(estimate∣no effect){\color{green}{P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{no effect})}}P(estimate∣no effect)
        • The significance level used in our hypothesis test

      • We don't know the others, but they can be calculated:
        • P(estimate∣effect){\color{navy}{P(\text{estimate}\mid\text{effect})}}P(estimate∣effect) is the statistical power of the test
